# Badge Voice Guide

A reference for writing Kismet badges. Use this when writing badges for new locations or expanding existing sets. The existing Kismet badge set is the gold standard — study it before writing anything new.

---

## The Core Philosophy

A Kismet badge is a specific moment, not a general aspiration.

The best badges describe something you either did or didn't do. Not something you're working toward or feeling or becoming — something that happened. You know when you've earned it. There's no ambiguity. There's a moment.

Badges should make people want to do the thing, not just want to check the box.

If a badge sounds like something a wellness app would say, rewrite it. If it sounds like a corporate goal-setting framework, rewrite it. If it sounds like something a real person would say to a friend about something worth doing — you're close.

---

## The Rules

**1. You know when you've done it.**
There must be a clear, unambiguous completion moment. Not "be more connected to nature" — that's not checkable. "Watch a full sunrise at the beach without looking at your phone" — that's checkable.

Good: "Cook a 3-course meal for the family"
Bad: "Develop a love of cooking"

**2. It's specific enough to have teeth.**
Vague badges feel hollow when you earn them. Add a minimum, a number, a constraint. The specificity is what makes it feel real.

Good: "Cook a meal completely from scratch (min. 3 items including a protein)"
Bad: "Cook a meal from scratch"

**3. It's interesting — you actually want to do it.**
If writing the badge doesn't make you want to go do the thing, the badge isn't working. A good badge is a prompt that makes you think "I should do that."

Good: "Go undefeated across a single game night"
Bad: "Win a game"

**4. No flowery language. No corporate wellness speak.**
Write simply. No "journey," no "embrace," no "explore your relationship with." Just say what the thing is.

Good: "Get caught in a rainstorm on the beach"
Bad: "Embrace the unpredictable beauty of nature"

**5. Bronze = try it. Silver = go deeper. Gold = this is part of who you are.**
The tier progression should feel like genuine escalation, not just repetition at higher volume. Bronze is access. Silver is commitment. Gold is identity.

---

## The Tier Progression Philosophy

**Bronze** — The first time. The version of the thing that most people could do if they decided to. Lower barrier, real experience.

**Silver** — You liked it enough to go back. Or it asks more of you. Or it's the same thing with real stakes.

**Gold** — This is now part of your life, your identity, or your story. It takes actual time, commitment, or achievement to unlock. Someone who has the gold badge would mention it if it came up. It means something.

The worst tier progressions are: "do it once / do it twice / do it three times." That's not escalation, it's repetition. Each tier should be a meaningfully different experience, not the same experience more times.

## Common Mistakes

**Too vague:** "Connect with your neighbors" — how do you know when you've done it?
Fix: "Invite a neighbor to dinner at your place."

**Too easy as gold:** "Watch a sunset" as gold tier — that's a bronze. Escalate.
Fix: "Watch a full sunrise and sunset on the same day" — now that's a day that means something.

**Corporate wellness voice:** "Prioritize your wellness by taking time for yourself"
Fix: "Take a full day off — no work, no to-do list, no catching up."

**Too location-specific for a universal badge:** "Walk the Brooklyn Bridge"
Fix: If this is a NYC badge, great. If it's supposed to be universal, it can't name a specific place.

**Weak silver:** Bronze = try it. Silver = try it again. That's not a progression.
Fix: Silver should require a qualitative leap, not just repetition.

**Over-explaining:** Don't write a paragraph. One sentence. Two if you need a specific qualifier.

## Five Great Badges (Annotated)

**"Go undefeated across a single game night"**
Why it works: It's hyper-specific (a single game night, not just "win a lot"), it's competitive without being aggressive, it's funny — everyone knows how hard this is, and it's checkable. There's one night where either you did or you didn't.

**"Cook a meal completely from scratch (min. 3 items including a protein)"**
Why it works: The parenthetical adds teeth. Without it, "from scratch" is vague enough to mean anything. The parenthetical removes all ambiguity while still leaving room for interpretation. It also has a slight edge — it implies someone has tried to game this before.

**"Find a shell you identify with at the beach"**
Why it works: It's the most personal badge in the set. It's not competitive or skill-based — it's reflective. And it's specific in a way that doesn't constrain: you know when you've found it. It also has personality — it's the kind of thing only a certain kind of person would think to put in a badge set.

**"Experience a named storm at the Kismet house"**
Why it works: You cannot manufacture this. It requires showing up and luck. It has a once-in-a-lifetime feel. The specificity of "named storm" makes it feel epic. And the phrase "at the Kismet house" gives it place — it couldn't happen anywhere else.

**"Be on a first-name basis at The Inn and The Dive"**
Why it works: It's unmistakably local. It names real places. It captures a real social milestone — the regulars know your name. And the plural (both places) adds specificity. A tourist couldn't earn this.

## Five Bad Badges (Annotated)

**"Embrace the outdoors and find peace in nature"**
What's wrong: Completely uncheckable. When did you embrace it? How much peace counts? Sounds like it belongs in a meditation app.
Fix: "Spend a night outside — camping, a backyard, a roof, anywhere."

**"Be a good friend"**
What's wrong: This is not a badge, it's a value. There's no moment where you've done it. It will mean different things to everyone.
Fix: "Show up for a friend during a genuinely hard moment — not a text, actually show up."

**"Exercise regularly"**
What's wrong: No finish line. No specific action. "Regularly" is doing all the work and it's undefined.
Fix: "Train for and finish a race of any distance (5k counts)."

**"Try new foods" (as a gold badge)**
What's wrong: This is barely a bronze. Trying one new food is something you could do at a party by accident. Gold should be identity-level.
Fix: "Eat at 25 different restaurants in your neighborhood. Become the person people ask."

**"Live life to the fullest"**
What's wrong: This is a bumper sticker. It tells you nothing about what to do, measures nothing, and earns nothing. If you wrote this badge, start over.
Fix: Anything specific. Literally anything. "Take a solo trip for at least one night — just you" is one thousand times better.
